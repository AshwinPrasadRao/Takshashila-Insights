A great-power summit that sends a message to the middle powers

May 17, 2026 Anushka Saxena Times of India

Summary: The recent US-China summit underscored a deeply competitive relationship, with China advocating for "constructive strategic stability" to manage long-term rivalry rather than seeking harmony. While the US prioritized securing market access deals, genuine reciprocal concessions proved challenging, highlighting ongoing competition in critical sectors. A significant outcome for middle powers is the message that the US may be shifting away from its traditional alliance leadership, compelling them to pursue greater strategic autonomy and diversify partnerships.

Key Takeaways

  • The US-China relationship is defined by managed competition, where both powers accept rivalry but seek to keep it under control.
  • The US is increasingly prioritizing bilateral trade outcomes and direct engagement, potentially signaling a diminished role for its traditional alliance system.
  • Middle powers, like India, must prepare for an era where they are expected to navigate great-power competition with greater strategic autonomy and diversified partnerships, rather than relying solely on the US.

India’s First Digital Census: A geospatial blueprint for growth

May 18, 2026 Dr Y Nithiyanandam Money Control

Summary: India's first fully digital census presents a significant opportunity to establish a spatially informed and data-driven governance system, effectively creating a 'digital twin' of the country for economic planning and infrastructure development. However, the process faces several challenges, including an outdated questionnaire, technical glitches, limited public awareness, and inadequate support for citizens and enumerators. To realize its transformative potential, the census requires systemic improvements, a more user-friendly approach, better communication, and extended timelines to encourage greater public participation and ensure data accuracy.

Key Takeaways

  • India's digital census holds transformative potential to create a geospatial 'digital twin' for data-driven governance and planning across economic, infrastructure, and energy sectors.
  • Current implementation is hampered by archaic questions, technical inaccuracies, poor user experience, and low voluntary public participation, requiring significant improvements.
  • Addressing grievances, improving technology, enhancing communication, and extending timelines are crucial for the census to overcome its initial hurdles and fulfill its promise as a tool for nation-building and effective public engagement.
